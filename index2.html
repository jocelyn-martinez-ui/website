Holland Walking Tour

Role

Objective

Year

The Mission

Bridging the gap between a static past and a digital future.

The Holland Walking Tour App modernizes the way tourists experience Holland, Michigan. By transforming a static, physical map into an interactive and user-friendly digital platform, the app enhances accessibility, engagement, and convenience for visitors of all ages.

At its core, each element was designed with simplicity, clarity, and scalability in mind, ensuring both ease of use and future growth opportunities beyond the Tulip Festival.

Tourism Board

Focus: Modernizing walking tours to attract visitors year-round, not just during seasonal peak events.

Tulip Time Festival

Focus: High-traffic event logistics and providing real-time navigation for dense crowds.

Library Archivist

Focus: Maintaining accuracy of the local landmarks database and cultural storytelling.

Supervising Professor

Focus: Collaborating on the project framework, database design, and technical viability.

Design System

Visual Harmony & Accessibility

A successful experience is intuitive and readable in sunlight. To achieve this, I implemented a design system that prioritizes contrast and legibility for an intergenerational audience.

Typography Logic: We utilized a minimum 16px body font across the entire application. This was a non-negotiable requirement to ensure older users, often exploring in harsh outdoor lighting, could navigate without confusion or eye strain.

HERITAGE TEAL #008080
SKY MIST (60%) #B2EBF2
STONE GREY #D7D6D6
SLATE TEXT #2C333E

The Reasoning

The teal was selected to anchor the app in Holland’s coastal and Dutch heritage. The Slate (#2C333E) and Muted Blue (#929FB3) text palette provides high contrast ratios that meet WCAG accessibility standards, ensuring the app remains usable for everyone from tech-savvy teenagers to seniors.

Reflection

Closing Thoughts

Transitioning from a Computer Science background to UX design on this project taught me that technical perfection is meaningless if it isn't human-centric. My primary takeaway was the concept of **"Invisible Accessibility."**

By designing for the most restricted user (the senior exploring in direct sunlight), the experience became better for the most casual user (the distracted student on their phone). Moving forward, I aim to continue building tools that don't just "work," but feel like a natural extension of the user's environment.

Success Metric: Success is measured not just by app downloads, but by increased foot traffic to the "hidden gems" identified by the Business Association and positive feedback from the Library Archivist on the preservation of our city's stories.
