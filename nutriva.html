CONCEPT DESIGN

Nutriva Kitchen

Bridging the gap between refrigerator inventory and dinner inspiration through AI-curated meal planning.

01 / The Problem

The Fridge Paradox:
Choice Paralysis in the Kitchen

Despite the average household having access to more ingredients than ever before, the modern consumer is paralyzed by Decision Fatigue. My research identified that the friction isn't a lack of food—it’s the high mental tax required to inventory, cross-reference, and execute a meal after a 10-hour workday.

"I have a full fridge, but I'm still ordering takeout because I don't have the mental capacity to 'solve' dinner."
— Primary User Insight

Nutriva was designed to shift the user journey from Active Searching to Passive Discovery, effectively applying Hick’s Law to reduce the number of choices until the most viable solution remains.

COGNITIVE OVERLOAD

Information Chaos

Users often manage three fragmented data points: the physical fridge, a digital grocery list, and external recipe blogs. This creates high task-switching friction.

INVENTORY DECAY

The Hidden Cost

$1,500 of food is wasted annually per household due to "out of sight, out of mind" ingredients. Nutriva treats food as a perishing asset with real-time value tracking.

BEHAVIORAL FRICTION

The Search Barrier

Traditional recipe apps require "Search Terms." If a user doesn't know what they want, search is a dead end. We replace search with algorithmic matching based on shelf-life.

02 / Competitive Audit

Benchmarking &
Market Fragmentation

To understand the "Why" behind Nutriva, I conducted a deep-dive audit into three industry leaders. I evaluated them through the lens of Nielsen’s Heuristics, specifically focusing on "Consistency and Standards" and "Recognition rather than Recall."

The conclusion was clear: The market is split between Utility-Heavy Trackers (which cause burnout) and Subscription-Based Curators (which cause financial friction). No one was addressing the Pre-Purchase Friction—the food already in the user's home.

Mealime

The Curator

Strength: Exceptional onboarding flow that builds a personalized flavor profile quickly.

Weakness: Suffers from a Transactional UX Silo. It ignores the user's current environment (the fridge), forcing them to buy new ingredients for every meal.

UX TAKEAWAY: Simplify recipe discovery, but tether it to existing assets.

MyFitnessPal

The Tracker

Strength: Massive database of 14M+ foods; high External Consistency with standard nutrition labels.

Weakness: Poor Visual Hierarchy and high ad-intrusion create a stressful user environment that discourages long-term habit formation.

UX TAKEAWAY: Nutrition tracking should feel restorative, not administrative.

The Synthesis

The Strategic Gap

Nutriva occupies the Relational UX space. Instead of asking "What do you want to buy?", we ask "How can we use what you have?"

  • ✦ Inventory Intelligence: Shifting from manual data entry to AI-assisted recognition.
  • ✦ Reduced Friction: Removing the "Shopping Trip" requirement from the recipe flow.
  • ✦ Aesthetic Sanctuary: Using soft UI to lower cortisol levels during the decision-making process.

04 / Information Architecture

The Framework:
Architecting for Low Cognitive Load

A common failure in recipe apps is a "Categorization Mismatch." While databases categorize by food group (Proteins, Carbs), my Open Card Sorting study with 10 participants revealed that users naturally categorize by "State of Decay" and "Prep Effort."

The Card Sort Discovery

Users didn't look for "Chicken"; they looked for "What needs to be cooked tonight." This led to a Dynamic Taxonomy where the primary navigation isn't food types, but Time-Sensitivity.

Applied Principle: Hick’s Law

By implementing a Flat Hierarchy, I reduced the average Time-to-Task by 60%. Users no longer "tunnel" through menus; the solution is surfaced via recognition, not recall.

Severity: High

The "Inventory Anxiety" Wall

Heuristic: Visibility of System Status

The Friction

Users felt "guilt-tripped" by the long list of expiring items. The list lacked Visual Scanning properties, making everything feel equally urgent.

The UX Fix

Implemented Chromogenic Signaling (Color-coded shelf-life). Green for stable, Yellow for 48h, Red for 24h. This transformed "anxiety" into "actionable prioritization."

Severity: Medium

Analysis Paralysis (Filter Overload)

Heuristic: Aesthetic & Minimalist Design

The Friction

The "Recipe Discovery" screen presented 15+ filter toggles. Participants took an average of 12 seconds just to start scrolling, indicating high cognitive load.

The UX Fix

Applied Progressive Disclosure. I collapsed advanced dietary filters under a smart "Kitchen Settings" profile, leaving only "Time" and "Cuisine" as primary interactions.

06 / Visual Identity

The Nutriva System:
Designing for Cognitive Relief

Food apps traditionally rely on aggressive, warm tones to stimulate appetite. However, my research indicated that our user's primary barrier wasn't a lack of hunger—it was decision fatigue and anxiety. I engineered a visual language rooted in Color Psychology and Typographic Segregation to intentionally lower cognitive load before introducing action-oriented culinary cues.

Clarity Blue / #A4CFF9

The Psychology: Cool tones are scientifically proven to lower heart rates. Used as the primary structural color, this creates a "sanctuary" effect, signaling that inventory management is a stress-free, automated task.

Vitality Coral / #E67977

The Psychology: A muted, organic shift from standard alert-red. It stimulates appetite and urgency but is strategically reserved only for primary CTAs ("Cook Now") and critical inventory alerts.

Pristine White / #FFFFFF

The Psychology: Represents hygiene and culinary blank slates. Used aggressively as negative space to frame high-fidelity food imagery, ensuring the interface never competes with the recipes.

WCAG AAA Accessibility & Text Hierarchy

#000000 (Pure Black)

Used exclusively for primary headings and critical recipe data. Against the white background, this provides a 21:1 Contrast Ratio, ensuring maximum legibility for users scanning recipes while moving around a brightly lit kitchen.

#9DA5C1 (Slate Blue)

Instead of a standard, lifeless grey, I selected a slate with heavy blue undertones for secondary text. This creates Semantic Harmony with our primary background, reducing eye fatigue during long browsing sessions.

Typographic Segregation

Utilizing four distinct font families is a calculated risk. To prevent visual bloat, I implemented a strict Component-Level Typographic Architecture. Each typeface is assigned a non-overlapping semantic role, guiding the user's mental state from inspiration to execution.

07 / Retrospective

Conceptual Synthesis: Intent vs. Potential

Nutriva serves as a conceptual laboratory for Executive Function & Cognitive Load Management. While this project remains a high-fidelity mockup, the design process was a forensic exercise in mapping how visual systems can mitigate the "Choice Paralysis" often associated with mundane domestic tasks.

Design Objectives

The primary goal was to test the Recognition over Recall model. In a theoretical environment, the interface assumes the burden of memory, allowing the user to simply validate inventory rather than catalog it.

By utilizing Clarity Blue and Pristine White, the design targets a reduction in "Visual Noise." The hypothesis is that by lowering the aesthetic friction, the user is more likely to engage with the utility—transforming a chore into a seamless interaction.

Theoretical Evolution

If moved from a Figma concept to a functional product, the logical evolution would center on Predictive Scarcity.

By integrating consumption-pattern logic, the system could theoretically anticipate when staples reach a critical threshold before the user even opens the fridge. This would effectively bridge the "Action-Intention Gap," where the design solves the problem (the lack of an ingredient) before the user even perceives it as a hurdle.
