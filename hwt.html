Role

Objective

The Mission

Bridging the gap between a static past and a digital future.

The Holland Walking Tour App modernizes the way tourists experience Holland, Michigan. By transforming a static, physical map into an interactive and user-friendly digital platform, the app enhances accessibility, engagement, and convenience for visitors of all ages.

At its core, each element was designed with simplicity, clarity, and scalability in mind, ensuring both ease of use and future growth opportunities beyond the Tulip Festival.

Tourism Board

Focus: Modernizing walking tours to attract visitors year-round, not just during seasonal peak events.

Tulip Time Festival

Focus: High-traffic event logistics and providing real-time navigation for dense crowds.

Library Archivist

Focus: Maintaining accuracy of the local landmarks database and cultural storytelling.

Supervising Professor

Focus: Collaborating on the project framework, database design, and technical viability.

Design System

Visual Harmony & Accessibility

A successful experience is intuitive and readable in sunlight. To achieve this, I implemented a design system that prioritizes contrast and legibility for an intergenerational audience.

Typography Logic: We utilized a minimum 16px body font across the entire application. This was a non-negotiable requirement to ensure older users, often exploring in harsh outdoor lighting, could navigate without confusion or eye strain.

HERITAGE TEAL #008080
SKY MIST (60%) #B2EBF2

The Reasoning

The teal was selected to anchor the app in Holland’s coastal and Dutch heritage. The Slate (#2C333E) and Muted Blue (#929FB3) text palette provides high contrast ratios that meet WCAG accessibility standards, ensuring the app remains usable for everyone from tech-savvy teenagers to seniors.

System Design

The Curator’s Workbench: Scaling the Narrative

A primary challenge was ensuring the app remained a "living" document. For the Library Archivist, a static database was insufficient; they required a robust CMS to manage Holland's evolving historical records. I designed a management suite focused on relational scalability and cognitive ease.

Relational Data Mapping

To prevent data redundancy, I implemented a "Global Stop Library." This allows archivists to create a single historical record that can be dynamically linked to multiple different tours, ensuring data integrity across the entire platform.

Modular Scalability

The architecture was built to accommodate "Seasonal Overlays." The system can toggle visibility for specific stops during events like Tulip Time without requiring a full code redeploy, making it a sustainable tool for the city board.

Designing for Administrative Utility

The UX of the admin panel prioritizes Actionable Insights over raw data. By visualizing "Engagement Drop-off" points, the system alerts archivists to physical locations in the city that might have poor GPS reception or confusing signage.

Scalability Logic

Built using a "Hub and Spoke" model. New tours can be "spun up" in minutes by simply re-sequencing existing stops from the master library.

Ease of Use

The admin UI utilizes a "Draft-to-Live" workflow, reducing the risk of publishing unverified historical data to the public app.

Data Preservation

Every archival image is stored with high-fidelity metadata (Year, Photographer, Catalog ID) to satisfy Library of Congress standards.

Final Reflection

Synthesizing Logic, Heritage, and Human-Centricity

The Holland Walking Tour project served as a definitive bridge between my Computer Science background and my evolution into UX Design. It challenged me to move beyond "functional code" toward "meaningful systems." This project was a masterclass in solving for a multi-layered ecosystem: the senior citizen navigating physical streets, the library archivist preserving institutional memory, and the city board driving local commerce.

Inclusive Design as a Framework

By prioritizing "Invisible Accessibility," I addressed the high-friction constraints of our primary persona—seniors. Designing for high-glare environments and reduced cognitive load didn't just solve for one demographic; it created Universal Ease-of-Use. Whether it’s a distracted student or a tourist in mid-day sun, the interface remains resilient, proving that accessibility is simply good design for everyone.

Service Design & Institutional Stewardship

A mobile app is only as strong as the data that powers it. By designing the Curator’s Workbench, I addressed the "Back-of-House" UX. I empowered archivists to manage Holland's historical assets through a Relational Data Model, ensuring the system is not a static artifact, but a scalable platform that can grow with the city for decades to come.

The "Full-Stack" Designer Advantage

My Computer Science degree allowed me to approach this project with Technical Empathy. I understood the underlying constraints of GPS geofencing, API calls, and data persistence. This allowed me to design a system that is not only beautiful and empathetic but inherently buildable. I am a designer who speaks the language of development, ensuring that user needs are never lost in translation during handoff.
